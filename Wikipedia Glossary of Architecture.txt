"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baluster"" title=""Baluster"">Baluster</a>"|"
A small moulded shaft, square or circular, in stone or wood, sometimes metal, supporting the coping of a parapet or the handrail of a staircase. A series of balusters supporting a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handrail"" title=""Handrail"">handrail</a> or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coping_(architecture)"" title=""Coping (architecture)"">coping</a> is called a <b>balustrade</b>.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bar-stayed_girder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1"" class=""new"" title=""Bar-stayed girder (page does not exist)"">Bar-stayed girder</a>"|"
A structural member of inadequate capacity for its load or span that is augmented by one or two steel bars anchored to each bearing end at or above the centroid of the girder to assume the tension forces.  The bar(s) runs down and below the girder and stand off the girder on one or more struts anchored to the girder at its bottom surface.  The struts are sized to accept the compressive forces imposed without bending. The load limit to this member is the crippling capacity (horizontal failure) of the girder.

It was called <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotrachelium"" title=""Hypotrachelium"">hypotrachelium</a> by <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitruvius"" title=""Vitruvius"">Vitruvius</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column"" title=""Column"">Column</a>"|"
A structural element that transmits, through compression, the weight of the structure above to other structural elements below.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compluvium"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Compluvium"">Compluvium</a>"|"
Latin term for the open space left in the roof of the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atrium_(architecture)"" title=""Atrium (architecture)"">atrium</a> of a Roman house (<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domus"" title=""Domus"">domus</a>) for lighting it and the rooms round.<sup id=""cite_ref-13"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-13"">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>

The capping or covering of a wall.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corbel"" title=""Corbel"">Corbel</a>"|"
A structural piece of stone, wood or metal jutting from a wall to carry a superincumbent weight.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corinthian_order"" title=""Corinthian order"">Corinthian order</a>"|"
One of the three <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_order"" title=""Classical order"">orders</a> or organisational systems of Ancient Greek or classical architecture characterised by columns which stood on the flat pavement of a temple with a base, their vertical shafts fluted with parallel concave grooves topped by a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_(architecture)"" title=""Capital (architecture)"">capital</a> decorated with <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acanthus_(ornament)"" title=""Acanthus (ornament)"">acanthus</a> leaves, that flared from the column to meet an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abacus_(architecture)"" title=""Abacus (architecture)"">abacus</a> with concave sides at the intersection with the horizontal beam that they carried.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornice"" title=""Cornice"">Cornice</a>"|"
Upper section of an entablature, a projecting shelf along the top of a wall often supported by brackets or <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corbel"" title=""Corbel"">corbels</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cresting_(architecture)"" title=""Cresting (architecture)"">Cresting</a>"|"

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doric_order"" title=""Doric order"">Doric order</a>"|"
One of the three <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_order"" title=""Classical order"">orders</a> or organisational systems of Ancient Greek or classical architecture characterised by columns which stood on the flat pavement of a temple without a base, their vertical shafts fluted with parallel concave grooves topped by a smooth <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_(architecture)"" title=""Capital (architecture)"">capital</a> that flared from the column to meet a square <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abacus_(architecture)"" title=""Abacus (architecture)"">abacus</a> at the intersection with the horizontal beam that they carried.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dormer"" title=""Dormer"">Dormer</a>"|"
A structural element of a building that protrudes from the plane of a sloping <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof"" title=""Roof"">roof</a> surface. Dormers are used, either in original construction or as later additions, to create usable space in the roof of a building by adding headroom and usually also by enabling addition of windows.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flushwork"" title=""Flushwork"">Flushwork</a>"|"

"Gablets"|"
Triangular terminations to <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buttress"" title=""Buttress"">buttresses</a>, much in use in the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_English_Period"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Early English Period"">Early English</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decorated_Gothic"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Decorated Gothic"">Decorated periods</a>, after which the buttresses generally terminated in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinnacle"" title=""Pinnacle"">pinnacles</a>. The Early English gablets are generally plain, and very sharp in pitch. In the Decorated period they are often enriched with paneling and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crocket"" title=""Crocket"">crockets</a>. They are sometimes finished with small crosses, but more often with finials.<sup id=""cite_ref-39"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-39"">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>

Popular during the Italian Renaissance.<sup id=""cite_ref-40"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-40"">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>

The process in which the gallets or small splinters of stone are inserted in the joints of coarse masonry to protect the mortar joints. (Greek: γεῖσον – often interchangeable with <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornice"" title=""Cornice"">cornice</a>) the part of the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entablature"" title=""Entablature"">entablature</a> that projects outward from the top of the frieze in the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doric_order"" title=""Doric order"">Doric order</a> and from the top of the frieze course of the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_order"" title=""Ionic order"">Ionic</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corinthian_order"" title=""Corinthian order"">Corinthian</a> orders; it forms the outer edge of the roof on the sides of a structure with a sloped roof.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_dome"" title=""Geodesic dome"">Geodesic dome</a>"|"
A structure formed of straight wood or metal members between points (or nodes) on a circular sphere (or part thereof) that are ""pinned"" at each connection point to two or more other members that transfer loads imposed on the structure to the base of the structure. The geometric areas between individual members may support a ""skin"" if the structure is to be enclosed.  A ""regular"" geodesic structure have members of equal length but strengths of members may vary depending on location in the geodesic ""grid"".
"

"Interlaced arches"|"
A scheme of decoration employed in <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanesque_architecture"" title=""Romanesque architecture"">Romanesque</a> and <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothic_architecture"" title=""Gothic architecture"">Gothic</a> architecture, where <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch"" title=""Arch"">arches</a> are thrown from alternate <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pier_(architecture)"" title=""Pier (architecture)"">piers</a>, interlacing or intersecting one another. In the former case, the first arch <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molding_(decorative)"" title=""Molding (decorative)"">mould</a> is carried alternately over and under the second, in the latter the mouldings actually intersect and stop one another.<sup id=""cite_ref-43"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-43"">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_order"" title=""Ionic order"">Ionic order</a>"|"
One of the three <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_order"" title=""Classical order"">orders</a> or organisational systems of Ancient Greek or classical architecture characterised by columns which stood on the flat pavement of a temple with a base, their vertical shafts fluted with parallel concave grooves topped by a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_(architecture)"" title=""Capital (architecture)"">capital</a> with <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volute"" title=""Volute"">volutes</a>, that flared from the column to meet a rectangular <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abacus_(architecture)"" title=""Abacus (architecture)"">abacus</a> with carved <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovolo"" title=""Ovolo"">ovolo</a> moulding, at the intersection with the horizontal beam that they carried.

"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panelling"" title=""Panelling"">Panelling</a>"|"
Is a millwork wall covering constructed from rigid or semi-rigid components. These are traditionally interlocking wood, but could be plastic or other materials.

Panelling was developed in antiquity to make rooms in stone buildings more comfortable. The panels served to insulate the room from the cold stone. In more modern buildings, such panelling is often installed for decorative purposes. Panelling, such as wainscoting and boiserie in particular, may be extremely ornate and is particularly associated with seventeenth and eighteenth century interior design, Victorian architecture in Britain, and its international contemporaries.

Temple similar to a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#Dipteral"">dipteral</a> temple, in which the columns surrounding the naos have had walls built between them, so that they become engaged columns, as in the great temple at Agrigentum. In Roman temples, in order to increase the size of the celia, the columns on either side and at the rear became engaged columns, the portico only having isolated columns.<sup id=""cite_ref-56"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-56"">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
"
"Pteroma"|"
In Classical architecture, the enclosed space of a portico, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#Peristyle"">peristyle</a>, or stoa, generally behind a screen of columns.
"
"Pycnostyle"|"
Term given by <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitruvius"" title=""Vitruvius"">Vitruvius</a> to the intercolumniation between the columns of a temple, when this was equal to one and a half diameters.<sup id=""cite_ref-57"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-57"">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
"
"Quadriporticus"|"
Also known as a quadriportico – a four-sided <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portico"" title=""Portico"">portico</a>.  The closest modern parallel would be a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonnade"" title=""Colonnade"">colonnaded</a> <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrangle_(architecture)"" title=""Quadrangle (architecture)"">quadrangle</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quoin_(architecture)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Quoin (architecture)"">Quoin</a>"|"
The cornerstones of brick or stone walls.  Quoins are also common in some brickwork corners that are alternately recessed and expressed.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raking_cornice"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Raking cornice"">Rake</a>"|"
The diagonal outside facing edge of a gable, sometimes called a <i>raking cornice</i> or a <i>sloping cornice</i>.  Rake is equivalent to slope which is the ratio of the rise to the run of the roof.
"
"Rear vault"|"
<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vault_(architecture)"" title=""Vault (architecture)"">Vault</a> of the internal hood of a doorway or window to which a splay has been given on the reveal, sometimes the vaulting surface is terminated by a small rib known as the scoinson rib, and a further development is given by angle shafts carrying this rib, known as scoinson shafts.<sup id=""cite_ref-58"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-58"">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
"
"Return"|"
Receding edge of a flat face. On a flat signboard, for example, the return is the edge which makes up the board's depth.
"
"Revolving door"|"
An entrance door for excluding drafts from an interior of a building. A revolving door typically consists of three or four doors that hang on a center shaft and rotate around a vertical axis within a round enclosure.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rib_vault"" title=""Rib vault"">Rib vault</a>"|"
The intersection of two or three barrel vaults.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ridge_board&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1"" class=""new"" title=""Ridge board (page does not exist)"">Ridge board</a>"|"
A structural member that runs the length of the ridge (high point) on a sloped roof to which the upper ends of rafters are attached.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roof_comb"" title=""Roof comb"">Roof comb</a>"|"
The structure that tops a pyramid in monumental Mesoamerican architecture (also common as a decorative embellishment on the ridge of metal roofs of some domestic Gothic-style architecture in America in the 19th century).
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotunda_(architecture)"" title=""Rotunda (architecture)"">Rotunda</a>"|"
A large and high circular hall or room in a building, usually but not always, surmounted by a dome.


"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sash_window"" title=""Sash window"">Sash</a>"|"
The horizontal and vertical frame that encloses the glazing of a window.  A sash may be fixed or operable and may be of several different types depending on operation (i.e. casement, single or double hung, awning, hopper or sliding).
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_hall"" title=""Great hall"">Screens passage</a>"|"
The passage at one end of the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_hall"" title=""Great hall"">Great hall</a> of an English medieval house or castle, and separated from it by the spere.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scroll_(art)"" title=""Scroll (art)"">Scroll</a>"|"
Ornamental element featuring a sequence of spiraled, circled or heart shaped motifs. There are, among others, flower scrolls, foliated scrolls, plants scrolls, vines scrolls.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Site-specific_architecture"" title=""Site-specific architecture"">Site-specific architecture</a>"|"
Architecture which is of its time and of its place. It is designed to respond to both its physical context, and the metaphysical context within which it has been conceived and executed
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skeiling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1"" class=""new"" title=""Skeiling (page does not exist)"">Skeiling</a>"|"
A straight sloped part of a ceiling, such as on the underside of a pitched roof.
<sup id=""cite_ref-59"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-59"">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
"
"Sommer or Summer <span id=""sommer""></span><span id=""summer""></span>"|"
Girder or main ""summer beam"" of a floor: if supported on two storey posts and open below, also called a ""bress"" or ""breast-summer"". Often found at the centerline of the house to support one end of a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joist"" title=""Joist"">joist</a>, and to bear the weight of the structure above.<sup id=""cite_ref-60"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-60"">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spandrel"" title=""Spandrel"">Spandrel</a>"|"
The space between two arches or between an arch and a rectangular enclosure.&#160;&#160;In a building facade, the space between the top of the window in one story and the sill of the window in the story above.
"
"Spere"|"
The fixed structure between the great hall and the screens passage in an English medieval timber house.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spire"" title=""Spire"">Spire</a>"|"
A tapering conical or pyramidal structure on the top of a building.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Springer_(architecture)"" title=""Springer (architecture)"">Springer</a>"|"
The lowest <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voussoir"" title=""Voussoir"">voussoir</a> on each side of an arch.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squinch"" title=""Squinch"">Squinch</a>"|"
A piece of construction used for filling in the upper angles of a square room so as to form a proper base to receive an octagonal or spherical <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dome"" title=""Dome"">dome</a>.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagioscope"" title=""Hagioscope"">Squint</a>"|"
An opening, often arched, through an internal wall of a church providing an oblique view of the altar.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoop_(architecture)"" title=""Stoop (architecture)"">Stoop</a>"|"
A small <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staircase"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Staircase"">staircase</a> ending in a platform and leading to the entrance of an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apartment_building"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Apartment building"">apartment building</a> or other building.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunburst"" title=""Sunburst"">Sunburst</a>"|"
A design or figure commonly used in architectural ornaments and design patterns, including <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_nouveau"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Art nouveau"">art nouveau</a>.
"
"Systyle"|"
in the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_order"" title=""Classical order"">classical orders</a>, this describes columns rather thickly set, with an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercolumniation"" title=""Intercolumniation"">intercolumniation</a> to which two diameters are assigned.<sup id=""cite_ref-61"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_architecture#cite_note-61"">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timber_framing"" title=""Timber framing"">Timber framing</a>"|"
Is the method of creating structures using heavy timbers jointed by pegged <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortise_and_tenon"" title=""Mortise and tenon"">Mortise and tenon</a> joints.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trabeated_arch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1"" class=""new"" title=""Trabeated arch (page does not exist)"">Trabeated arch</a>"|"
An <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arch"" title=""Arch"">arch</a> pointed at the top formed by <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voussoir"" title=""Voussoir"">voussoirs</a> whose inside radius is greater than one-half the span of the arch.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracery"" title=""Tracery"">Tracery</a>"|"
The stonework elements that support the glass in a Gothic window.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transom_(architectural)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Transom (architectural)"">Transom (architectural)</a>"|"
Window or element, fixed or operable,  above a door but within its vertical frame.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truss"" title=""Truss"">Truss</a>"|"
A structural component made of straight wood or metal members, usually in a triangular pattern, with ""pinned"" connections at the top and bottom chords and which is used to support structural loads, as those on a floor, roof or bridge.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turret"" title=""Turret"">Turret</a>"|"
A small tower that projects vertically from the wall of a building such as a medieval castle.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)"" title=""Tympanum (architecture)"">Tympanum</a>"|"
(<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_(language)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Greek (language)"">Greek</a> τύμπανον, from τύπτειν, to strike) the triangular space enclosed between the horizontal cornice of the entablature and the sloping cornice of the pediment. Though sometimes left plain, it is often decorated.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undercroft"" title=""Undercroft"">Undercroft</a>"|"
Traditionally, a cellar or storage room.  In modern usage, a ground-level area that is relatively open to the sides, but covered by the building above.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ventilation_shaft"" title=""Ventilation shaft"">Ventilation shaft</a>"|"
A small, vertical space within a tall building which permits ventilation of the building.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truss#Vierendeel_Truss"" title=""Truss"">Vierendeel truss</a>"|"
A rectilinear truss usually fabricated of steel or concrete with horizontal top and bottom chords and vertical web members (no diagonals) in which the loads imposed on it are transferred to the supports through bending forces resisted in its connections.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volute"" title=""Volute"">Volute</a>"|"
A spiral, scroll-like ornament that forms the basis of the Ionic order.
"
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voussoir"" title=""Voussoir"">Voussoir</a>"|"
A wedge-shaped or tapered stone between the springer and the keystone used to construct an arch.
